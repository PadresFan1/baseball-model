Executive Summary

Over the course of this optimization cycle, we moved away from a fragile, overfitted backtest and built an institutional-grade, leak-free machine learning pipeline. By testing across a 4-fold expanding window (2022–2025) and optimizing strictly for log-loss using four core metrics (d_woba, d_xwoba_bat, d_xfip, d_xwoba_pit), we discovered exactly how your model behaves when facing real market prices:

- The Model is an Underdog Sniper: Testing with flat $20 bets revealed that your model isolates deep underdog value. It maintains a near-flat 40% win rate across the board, but because it targets long plus-money odds, it hovers near break-even (-1.6% ROI).
- The Threshold Sweep Proof: When you tightened the edge filter to >12%, your win rate stayed pinned at 39.5%, but your profits surged to +6.1% ROI. This proved the model isn't getting "smarter" or more accurate at higher thresholds—it is simply selecting longer odds where its 40% win rate mathematically exploits the market.
- The Regularization Negative Result: Dropping your L2 regularization penalty from C=1.0 to C=0.1 yielded nearly identical results (bet volume and win rate barely budged). This was our most critical diagnostic signal: the model's overconfidence is a structural data issue, not a math variance issue.
- The 2024 Blind Spot: The model consistently suffers severe blowouts in 2024 because it relies on raw, cumulative metrics. It completely missed the massive league-wide offensive suppression of 2024, causing it to aggressively back bad underdogs into dominant favorite pitching.

Your Two Structural Options Moving Forward
To break through the 40% win-rate ceiling and protect your bankroll from single-season environment shifts, we must change the data entering the engine. Here are your two pathways:

Option 1: Season-Normalized Relative Features 
Instead of feeding the model raw differences (e.g., Home $wOBA - \text{Away } wOBA$), we rewrite the feature pipeline to scale every team metric against the rolling league average for that specific calendar day.

How it works: A team's $wOBA$ becomes a percentage of the league baseline (e.g., a $wOBA$ of $.330$ in a high-offense year might be $102\%$ of league average, but in low-offense 2024 it might be $110\%$).

The Benefit: This completely insulates the model from year-over-year run environment changes, fixing the 2024 blowout entirely.

Option 2: The "Market-Residual" Approach
Instead of forcing the model to calculate a win probability entirely from scratch, we include the sportsbook's de-vigged market probability as a core baseline feature in the logistic regression.

How it works: The model's job changes. It accepts the market price as a reflection of today's real-time lineup changes, player injuries, and bullpen availability. Your four Statcast features are then used strictly to predict whether the market has mispriced the underlying team skills relative to that baseline.

The Benefit: This prevents the model from walking into obvious "stale metric traps" where an underdog looks great on paper but is missing its best players in reality.
